## I. Today's Reading

**NKJV:** 1 Corinthians 10:14–22

**14** Therefore, my beloved, flee from idolatry.

**15** I speak as to wise men; judge for yourselves what I say.

**16** The cup of blessing which we bless, is it not the communion of the blood of Christ? The bread which we break, is it not the communion of the body of Christ?

**17** For we, though many, are one bread and one body; for we all partake of that one bread.

**18** Observe Israel after the flesh: Are not those who eat of the sacrifices partakers of the altar?

**19** What am I saying then? That an idol is anything, or what is offered to idols is anything?

**20** Rather, that the things which the Gentiles sacrifice they sacrifice to demons and not to God, and I do not want you to have fellowship with demons.

**21** You cannot drink the cup of the Lord and the cup of demons; you cannot partake of the Lord's table and of the table of demons.

**22** Or do we provoke the Lord to jealousy? Are we stronger than He?

---

## II. Key Summary

- Paul says "flee" — not "be careful" — meaning the goal isn't to find the boundary but to move in the opposite direction entirely
- Eating at a pagan temple in Corinth wasn't just socializing; it was forming a real spiritual bond with the demonic
- The Lord's table and the table of demons cannot coexist — loyalty in faith only goes one way

---

## III. Passage Analysis

### "Flee" Means Run, Not Calculate

> "Do not be trying how near you can get to it, but rather how far you can get from it." [1]

Paul doesn't say "watch yourself around idolatry" — he says flee. The point isn't figuring out how close you can stand to the fire without getting burned; it's turning and going the other way. Maximum distance, not minimum safe distance.

---

### Where You Eat Declares Who You Belong To

> "The Greek word for 'partakers' (koinonia) in verses 18–20 is the same word for 'communion' and 'fellowship,' meaning that eating at a pagan temple meal creates real spiritual fellowship with demons, not merely an innocent social meal." [2]

The word koinonia — "partakers" — is the exact same word used for communion with Christ. Paul is saying: who you sit with at the table isn't just a social choice; it's a spiritual statement about where your loyalty actually belongs.

---

## IV. Reflection Question

Have you ever told yourself "I'll just go watch, I won't actually do anything" — and then ended up going along with things you didn't plan to, because once you were there it was hard to be the only one who didn't?

If that pull felt stronger than you expected, maybe Paul's word "flee" is saying: the time to turn around is before you show up, not after.
