## I. Today's Reading

**NKJV:** Ruth 4:7-12

**7** Now this was the custom in former times in Israel concerning redeeming and exchanging, to confirm anything: one man took off his sandal and gave it to the other, and this was a confirmation in Israel.

**8** Therefore the close relative said to Boaz, "Buy it for yourself." So he took off his sandal.

**9** And Boaz said to the elders and all the people, "You are witnesses this day that I have bought all that was Elimelech's, and all that was Chilion's and Mahlon's, from the hand of Naomi.

**10** Moreover, Ruth the Moabitess, the widow of Mahlon, I have acquired as my wife, to perpetuate the name of the dead through his inheritance, that the name of the dead may not be cut off from among his brethren and from his position at the gate. You are witnesses this day."

**11** And all the people who were at the gate, and the elders, said, "We are witnesses. The Lord make the woman who is coming to your house like Rachel and Leah, the two who built the house of Israel; and may you prosper in Ephrathah and be famous in Bethlehem.

**12** May your house be like the house of Perez, whom Tamar bore to Judah, because of the offspring which the Lord will give you from this young woman."

---

## II. Key Summary

- The nearer kinsman removed his sandal and handed it to Boaz — an ancient custom that publicly confirmed the legal transfer of the right of redemption, sealing everything in a single gesture.
- Boaz declared before the elders that he had purchased Elimelech's entire estate and taken Ruth as his wife — every promise he had made was now publicly sealed.
- The elders and all the people gave Ruth the highest blessing they knew: they compared her to Rachel and Leah, folding this outsider from Moab fully into Israel's own story.

---

## III. Passage Analysis

### One Sandal, One Sealed Promise

> "The man resigns the right of walking on the land as master, in favour of him to whom he gives the shoe." This was "a long-established custom... admitted for sufficient evidence in all such cases" — not written law, but universally recognized as legally binding. [1]

No paperwork, no lengthy process — just a sandal removed and handed over. Every promise made at the threshing floor became legally real in that single moment. Sometimes the most significant commitments are sealed in the simplest gestures.

---

### An Outsider Welcomed In

> "Rachel and Leah were 'of a foreign and heathenish original' yet became the matriarchs through whom the house of Israel was built. Invoking their names for Ruth was 'the intensest desire that Israelites could cherish in reference to an esteemed sister.'" [2]

The people at the gate didn't call her "Ruth the Moabitess" in their blessing — they held her up next to Rachel and Leah, women who also came from outside and became the foundation of everything. In that moment, the whole community welcomed Ruth into Israel's story. She was no longer an outsider.

## IV. Reflection Question

Have you ever been in a group or place where you weren't sure if you really belonged — if you were actually "one of them"?

Ruth came from Moab, yet the whole city blessed her with Israel's highest honor. If you've ever felt like you didn't fit anywhere, what do you think this story might be saying to you?
