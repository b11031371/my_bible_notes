## I. Today's Reading

**NKJV:** 1 Corinthians 15:29-34

**29** Otherwise, what will they do who are baptized for the dead, if the dead do not rise at all? Why then are they baptized for the dead?

**30** And why do we stand in jeopardy every hour?

**31** I affirm, by the boasting in you which I have in Christ Jesus our Lord, I die daily.

**32** If, in the manner of men, I have fought with beasts at Ephesus, what advantage is it to me? If the dead do not rise, "Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die!"

**33** Do not be deceived: "Evil company corrupts good habits."

**34** Awake to righteousness, and do not sin; for some do not have the knowledge of God. I speak this to your shame.

---

## II. Key Summary

- Paul points to the practice of baptism for the dead to argue that resurrection must be real — otherwise such practices make no sense (v. 29)
- Paul states he "dies daily," meaning his life was in constant physical danger, and asks: without resurrection, why would any of this be worth it? (vv. 30-32)
- Paul warns that bad company corrupts character and calls believers to "awake to righteousness," guarding against those whose denial of resurrection quietly reshapes their values (vv. 33-34)

---

## III. Passage Analysis

### A Hope Worth Dying For

> "Paul lived such a committed Christian life, people could look at him and say, 'There is no way he would live like that unless there was a reward waiting for him in heaven.' Most of us are so concerned about living comfortable lives here on earth that our lives give no evidence of the resurrection." [1]

When Paul says "I die daily," he is not speaking poetically — he literally had enemies who plotted to kill him. His willingness to face that kind of danger only makes sense if he truly believed death was not the end. The passage quietly asks us the same question: if resurrection is real, does it show in how we live today?

### Who You Spend Time With Shapes What You Start to Believe

> "Even evil words are dangerous. The constant repetition of an immoral maxim may lead to immoral life." [2]

Paul is not just giving general advice about choosing good friends. He is addressing people who denied the resurrection and lived accordingly — "eat, drink, for tomorrow we die." When you spend time around people who believe nothing matters beyond today, that mindset seeps in slowly and starts changing your choices without you noticing.

---

## IV. Reflection Question

Have you ever found yourself starting to think "who cares, just do what feels good now" — because everyone around you seemed to live that way?

Paul's point is that the resurrection is real, which means every choice you make now actually matters and will not just disappear. That is what gives you the courage to live differently from the crowd.
