## II. Key Summary

- God tells Ezekiel to take up a lamentation for Tyre, a city that had said of itself, "I am perfect in beauty."

- The passage pictures Tyre as a magnificent ship: planks of fir from Senir, a mast of cedar from Lebanon, oars of oak from Bashan, a sail of embroidered linen from Egypt; oarsmen from Sidon and Arvad, seam-caulkers from Gebal, and soldiers from Persia, Lydia, and Libya.

- Verses 12-25 list the nations trading with Tyre and the goods they exchanged—silver, iron, tin, lead, horses, ivory, precious stones, wheat, honey, oil, spices, and gold—and verse 13 records that Javan, Tubal, and Meshech bartered human lives for her merchandise.

---

## III. Passage Analysis

### "I Am Perfect in Beauty"

> Tyre became proud because of her economic prosperity and the beauty of her natural setting. In the same way, when a person does not treasure the gospel of truth as beautiful but boasts instead in "the lust of the flesh and the lust of the eyes," that person falls into an empty self-admiration that drifts away from God. [1]

Tyre's pride didn't come from nowhere—she really was beautiful and really was wealthy. The trouble started when she made those things her name tag, admired herself long enough to believe it, and left no room for God.

---

### When People Become Merchandise

> Among Tyre's merchandise is a tragic line: they trafficked in human slaves, treating people like mere goods—a practice that violated God's law and the dignity of those made in His image. [2]

On that glittering inventory, "human lives" sits in the same row as bronze, horses, and spices. Where usefulness and price are the only measures that count, people end up with a price tag too.

What do you most often point to as proof that you're doing okay?
