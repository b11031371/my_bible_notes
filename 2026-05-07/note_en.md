## I. Today's Reading

**NKJV:** Ruth 4:1-6

**1** Now Boaz went up to the gate and sat down there; and behold, the close relative of whom Boaz had spoken came by. So Boaz said, "Come aside, friend, sit down here." So he came aside and sat down.

**2** And he took ten men of the elders of the city, and said, "Sit down here." So they sat down.

**3** Then he said to the close relative, "Naomi, who has come back from the country of Moab, sold the piece of land which belonged to our brother Elimelech.

**4** And I thought to inform you, saying, 'Buy it back in the presence of the inhabitants and the elders of my people. If you will redeem it, redeem it; but if you will not redeem it, then tell me, that I may know; for there is no one but you to redeem it, and I am next after you.'" And he said, "I will redeem it."

**5** Then Boaz said, "On the day you buy the field from the hand of Naomi, you must also buy it from Ruth the Moabitess, the wife of the dead, to perpetuate the name of the dead through his inheritance."

**6** And the close relative said, "I cannot redeem it for myself, lest I ruin my own inheritance. You redeem my right of redemption for yourself, for I cannot redeem it."

---

## II. Key Summary

- Boaz was at the city gate first thing in the morning, ten elders assembled as witnesses — he had made a promise the night before and wasted no time keeping it.
- The nearer kinsman was ready to buy the field, but the moment he heard Ruth was part of the deal, he backed out: "I cannot redeem it for myself."
- He stepped aside, and Boaz stepped in. Same situation, two different hearts — and that difference determined who would carry the story forward.

---

## III. Passage Analysis

### Boaz at the Gate — He Showed Up

> "Boaz positioned himself at the gate deliberately, knowing the nearer kinsman would pass through. The kinsman's appearance exactly when needed demonstrates God's orchestration of events, allowing the matter to begin formally before public witnesses." [1]

The night before, Boaz had told Ruth he would not rest until the matter was settled. Dawn came, and he was already at the gate, waiting. He didn't know when — or even if — the kinsman would arrive. But he showed up anyway. God's timing often moves through people who keep their word.

---

### Same Choice, Two Different Hearts

> "The kinsman's self-interested calculation reveals his character starkly: he considers only what benefits himself — 'lest I ruin my own inheritance.' Boaz acts from genuine love, exemplifying redemptive action motivated by compassion rather than personal gain." [2]

One situation, two men: redeem the land, marry Ruth, carry on a dead man's name. One tallied up the cost and said no. The other had already decided to say yes. The difference wasn't in the terms — it was in what each man cared about most.

---

## IV. Reflection Question

Have you ever had a moment where you could have stepped up for someone — but after thinking about what it would cost you, you stayed quiet instead?

The unnamed kinsman's "no" made room for Boaz's "yes." If every chance to help someone carries meaning, what do you think that means?
