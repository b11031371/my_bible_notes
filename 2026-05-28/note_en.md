## I. Today's Reading

**NKJV:** 1 Corinthians 9:19–27

**19** For though I am free from all men, I have made myself a servant to all, that I might win the more;

**20** and to the Jews I became as a Jew, that I might win Jews; to those who are under the law, as under the law, that I might win those who are under the law;

**21** to those who are without law, as without law (not being without law toward God, but under law toward Christ), that I might win those who are without law;

**22** to the weak I became as weak, that I might win the weak. I have become all things to all men, that I might by all means save some.

**23** Now this I do for the gospel's sake, that I may be partaker of it with you.

**24** Do you not know that those who run in a race all run, but one receives the prize? Run in such a way that you may obtain it.

**25** And everyone who competes for the prize is temperate in all things. Now they do it to obtain a perishable crown, but we for an imperishable crown.

**26** Therefore I run thus: not with uncertainty. Thus I fight: not as one who beats the air.

**27** But I discipline my body and bring it into subjection, lest, when I have preached to others, I myself should become disqualified.

---

## II. Key Summary

- Paul was completely free, yet he chose to make himself a servant to all — not because he had to, but because winning people to the gospel mattered more to him than personal comfort or rights.
- He adapted his approach for different people without ever changing the message itself; what shifted was how he came alongside others, not what he stood for.
- Paul uses an athletic metaphor to describe the life of faith: run with direction, fight with purpose, and discipline yourself — otherwise you risk telling others how to live while quietly drifting away yourself.

---

## III. Passage Analysis

### Freedom That Chooses to Serve

> "Paul freed himself from everybody, just that he might be everybody's servant." [1]

Paul emphasizes that his servanthood was a choice, not a burden. Real freedom isn't just "I can do whatever I want" — it's "I know what matters most, so I can gladly let go of lesser things." He found that reaching people with the gospel was worth more than holding onto his own convenience or reputation.

### Running Like You Mean It

> "Athletes pursued a corruptible crown — pine leaves worth almost nothing — with tremendous effort, while Christians often show apathy toward an imperishable crown." [2]

Paul says he runs "not with uncertainty" and fights "not as one who beats the air" — his life has a clear aim. He also disciplines his body and keeps it under control, because he knows that habits and desires can quietly pull a person off course. Living with intention means actively choosing, day after day, to keep what truly matters in first place.

---

## IV. Reflection Question

Have you ever changed how you act around a certain friend group — the way you talk, what you laugh at, what you say you believe — just so they'd accept you? What did that feel like afterward?

Paul adapted everything about his approach, but he always knew *why* — if your own way of fitting in had a clear "why" behind it, what would that be?
