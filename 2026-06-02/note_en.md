---

## II. Key Summary

- The Corinthian gatherings were doing more harm than good — the rich-poor divide at their common meals was tearing apart the very community the Lord's Supper was meant to build.
- Paul grounds the institution of the Lord's Supper in direct revelation from Jesus himself, with the command "do this in remembrance of Me" — not as empty ritual but as a deliberate, heartfelt response to what Christ did.
- Every time believers eat the bread and drink the cup, they publicly proclaim Christ's death until He comes — so how we act at the table is part of the message we're sending.

---

## III. Passage Analysis

### How You Treat Others Determines Whether Your Gathering Counts

> "The ordinances of Christ, if they do not make us better, will be apt to make us worse." [1]

The Corinthians weren't skipping church — they showed up every week. But the wealthy ate their fill while the poor went hungry. Paul says this gathering "is not to eat the Lord's Supper." When the outward form is there but love is missing, the ritual doesn't just fail — it actually causes harm. Going through the motions without caring for the people around you turns worship into something that works against you.

### Taking Communion Is a Public Statement

> "Taking communion proclaims the Lord's death to God, to the devil, and to the world watching — so we should ask ourselves what sermon we're preaching by our conduct at the table." [2]

Paul says every time we eat this bread and drink this cup, we *proclaim* the Lord's death until He comes. That word "proclaim" is public, active, intentional. Communion isn't just a private spiritual moment — it's a declaration. And the way we treat the people sitting next to us is part of what we're declaring.

---

## IV. Reflection Question

Have you ever been at a group dinner, class activity, or church gathering where you were so caught up in your own circle that someone else ended up left out — did you notice at the time, and what did you do?

Paul told the Corinthians that what they were doing didn't count as the Lord's Supper at all, because the love was gone. If the form is right but the heart isn't, does it still count — and what would it take to change that?
