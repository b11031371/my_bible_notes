## II. Key Summary

- Through Ezekiel, God speaks to the prince of Tyre: because his heart was lifted up, he claimed "I am a god, I sit in the seat of gods," but God reminds him he is only a man, not a god (vv. 1-2).
- The prince of Tyre gained great riches—gold and silver stored in his treasuries—through his own wisdom and trade, and his heart grew even prouder because of that wealth (vv. 4-5).
- God announces He will bring the most terrible of the nations against him: he will be thrown down into the Pit, die in the midst of the seas, and finally be turned to ashes on the ground in the sight of all who watch (vv. 7-10, 16-19).

---

## III. Passage Analysis

### Pride is really just forgetting where it all came from

> When individuals or nations rely too much on their own wisdom, ability, and wealth, they often mistakenly think they possess unlimited power and treat themselves as gods. [1]

The prince of Tyre's problem wasn't that he was rich or that he was smart. It was that he treated all of it as his own achievement and forgot it was ever given to him. Once someone believes "I built all this by myself," it's an easy slide into looking down on everyone else.

---

### No matter how great you feel, you're still just a person

> The king may imagine himself enthroned among the gods, but God sees reality for what it is, not what egomaniacs perceive it to be. [2]

How you see yourself in your head is one thing; who you actually are is another. However good the prince of Tyre felt about himself, God saw him clearly—a person who could fail, who could fall, and who would one day die.

---

## IV. Reflection Question

When you ace a test, win a game, or pull something off, what's the first thought that pops into your head—"I'm the reason this worked," or do you think about the people who helped you along the way?

The prince of Tyre chalked every success up to himself and forgot he was only a man; try stopping to ask how much of your ability and your chances were actually handed to you rather than made by you.
