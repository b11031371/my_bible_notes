## II. Key Summary

- Freedom is real, but Paul reframes the question: not "am I allowed to do this?" but "will this help others grow?"
- Someone else's conscience can matter more than your rights. Sometimes, stepping back out of care for others is the most loving thing you can do.
- "Do all to the glory of God" (v.31) is the bottom line — a principle that applies to every choice, big or small.

---

## III. Passage Analysis

### 1. Freedom Finds Its Direction in Love

> "He is being very consistent according to one principle: liberty within the limits of love." [1]

Paul doesn't want to take freedom away — he wants to show us what freedom looks like when it's guided by love. When using your rights might cause someone else to stumble, love takes priority over being right. That's not a restriction on freedom; it's freedom living the way it was meant to.

### 2. Every Ordinary Moment Can Be an Act of Worship

> "No act of life is in itself either religious or secular. The quality of each act depends on the spirit which guides it." [2]

Eating, making choices, how you treat people — none of these are automatically "spiritual" or "not spiritual." What makes any moment count is the intention behind it. When verse 31 says "whatever you do," it's stretching this principle into every corner of daily life.

---

## IV. Reflection Question

Have you ever kept doing something you had every right to do — watching a show, texting, playing a game — while someone nearby was clearly being left out? Did you notice, and what did you do?

If the real question isn't "can I do this?" but "is this good for them?" — would your answer to that moment change?
